CHAPTER 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Hiring in India is broken on both sides. Companies spend 45 to 60 days and 15 to 25 percent of annual salary through agencies to fill a single technical role, with no guarantee that the person is actually good. AI companies have it worse — they need hundreds of domain experts (doctors to verify medical AI, lawyers to check legal outputs, engineers to review code) and no platform can find, test, and manage that kind of talent at scale.

On the other side, millions of skilled Indians are shut out of good opportunities. A talented developer in a tier-2 city gets filtered out because of their college name. A doctor with 10 years of experience has no way to enter the AI data economy. The systems that connect talent with work are outdated and biased.

TalentLens solves this. We are building a platform that uses AI-powered video interviews to objectively test candidates, then connects the verified ones with companies through a managed marketplace. We operate three business lines: an AI interview and assessment system, a talent marketplace for contract hiring, and a managed service where we build and run teams of domain experts for AI companies.

Our revenue comes from a 30 to 35 percent commission on every hour a contractor works through us. The model is simple — more contractors working more hours means more revenue, and it recurs every month. We need approximately Rs 25 lakhs to start. We break even at around 30 active contractors, which we expect to reach by Month 7 or 8 of operations. With a realistic ramp-up to about 200 active contractors by year end, we project Year 1 revenue of approximately Rs 1.1 crores.

Why now? India's AI market is at $14.3 billion and growing (VynZ Research, 2025). The gig workforce has crossed 12 million and is expanding at 21 percent per year, the fastest in the world (DemandSage, 2026). And despite all this talent, 82 percent of employers still cannot find the people they need (CXO Today, 2026). The gap is not a shortage of talent. It is a failure of the systems that match talent with opportunity. That is exactly what we fix.

2.3 The Problem

On the company side, the hiring process is slow, expensive, and unreliable. The average time to fill a technical role in India is 45 to 60 days. Recruitment agencies charge 15 to 25 percent of the candidate's annual salary — for a Rs 12 lakh position, that is Rs 1.8 to 3 lakhs spent just to find one person. And even after spending that, there is no quality guarantee. HireRight's Global Benchmark Report found that a significant portion of resumes contain exaggerated or false information.

AI companies face a harder version of this. Building and improving AI models requires genuine human expertise. Doctors need to check if the AI's medical advice is correct. Lawyers need to verify legal summaries. Software engineers need to review AI-generated code. These are not simple data entry tasks. AI companies often need hundreds of such experts on short notice, and no existing platform is set up to source, test, and manage that kind of workforce.

On the talent side, India produces millions of graduates every year across engineering, medicine, law, and other fields. Many of them are genuinely skilled, but they face barriers that have nothing to do with ability. A developer in a small city does not have the LinkedIn network to find remote work with a global company. A candidate from a lesser-known college gets filtered out before anyone looks at what they can actually do. Freelancers deal with payment delays, scope disputes, and no formal protections. And domain experts — doctors, researchers, senior engineers — who could earn well in the AI data economy have no structured way to enter it.

The problem is not that India lacks talent. The problem is that the systems connecting talent with opportunity are broken. TalentLens is designed to fix this.

2.4 The Solution

TalentLens operates through three connected business lines.

The first is AI Interview and Assessment. When a candidate signs up, they take a 20-minute video interview conducted by our AI system. The questions are tailored to their claimed skills — a software developer gets coding and system design questions, a doctor gets clinical case scenarios, a content writer gets language and reasoning tasks. The system scores each candidate on four parameters:

| Parameter | Weightage |
|:---|:---|
| Technical Knowledge | 40% |
| Problem-Solving Approach | 25% |
| Communication Clarity | 20% |
| Professionalism | 15% |

The candidate receives a TalentLens Score out of 100, which becomes their verified profile on the platform. The AI does not care about college names or personal connections. It evaluates what the person can actually do.

The second is the Talent Marketplace. Once candidates have verified scores, companies can search the talent pool by skill, domain, experience level, and score. They can hire contractors for anything from two weeks to twelve months. We handle the contract, time tracking, weekly reports, and payments. The company gets one invoice from us. The contractor gets guaranteed on-time payment. Both sides avoid the hassles of traditional freelance arrangements.

The third is AI Data Services, which is the highest-value part. AI companies need organized teams of domain experts for specific tasks — labeling medical images, evaluating chatbot responses, reviewing legal documents, creating scoring rubrics. We take complete responsibility. If a client needs 25 radiologists for three months, we find the right people from our pool, verify their credentials, onboard them, manage their daily output, audit quality, and deliver the finished work. The client works with us, not with 25 individual people.

2.5 Revenue Model

We charge a 30 to 35 percent markup on the contractor's hourly rate. If a contractor's base rate is Rs 600 per hour, the client pays us Rs 790 to 810 per hour. The contractor gets their full rate, and we keep Rs 190 to 210 per hour.

This works well because the revenue is recurring — it comes in every month as long as contractors are working. It grows naturally with the talent pool. And we do not need to keep selling new products. More contractors working more hours means more revenue.

CHAPTER 3: MARKET DEMAND

3.1 Industry Overview and Key Trends

Three large trends are creating demand for what TalentLens offers.

The first is that AI development needs human experts at scale. Every time a company like OpenAI, Google, or Meta trains a new model, it needs thousands of humans to label data, evaluate outputs, and verify quality. Medical AI needs doctors. Legal AI needs lawyers. Coding AI needs engineers. As models get more sophisticated, the quality bar goes up — companies need more skilled people, not just more people. The Indian AI market alone was valued at $14.3 billion in 2026 (VynZ Research, 2025).

The second is the rapid growth of India's gig economy. The gig workforce crossed 12 million workers in 2025 and is projected to reach 23.5 million by 2030, growing at 21 percent per year — the fastest of any country (DemandSage, 2026). Over 15 million freelancers are active on digital platforms. Workers and companies in India are increasingly comfortable with contract-based arrangements, which is a direct tailwind for a platform like ours.

The third trend is a paradox. Despite having one of the world's largest graduate populations, 82 percent of employers in India reported difficulty filling positions in 2026, with AI-related skills being the hardest to find (CXO Today, 2026). The India Skills Report 2026 found an employability rate of 56.35 percent — more than half of graduates are employable, but companies still cannot find them efficiently. This tells us the problem is clearly in the matching system, not in the talent supply.

5.4 Sustaining Competitive Advantage

We plan to protect our position over time in several ways. We will file for patent protection on the AI interview scoring algorithm once it is validated at scale. We will keep improving the AI engine by training it on a growing volume of interview data and client feedback — the more interviews we conduct, the better the system gets, and that is hard for someone new to catch up with. We will refine our data labeling quality processes with every project to bring down error rates and turnaround times.

On the marketing side, we will publish hiring trend reports and case studies regularly to build authority in the space. On the client side, dedicated account managers, free pilots, and weekly reports will build trust and make switching to another vendor inconvenient. On the contractor side, we will earn loyalty through fair pay, on-time payments, and a reputation system that rewards their best work.

5.5 Operations and Quality Control

Day-to-day work runs in three streams: talent operations (processing interviews, reviewing scores, managing the database), client operations (sales, account management, contracts), and project delivery (running data labeling projects, coordinating expert teams, auditing quality).

Quality is central to everything. We calibrate the AI interview scores regularly by comparing them against human expert evaluations and correcting any bias. Every client rates every contractor after each engagement, and contractors who consistently score below 3.0 out of 5 are removed. For data labeling projects, a separate quality reviewer checks 10 to 15 percent of all output. If any batch exceeds a 5 percent error rate, it goes back for correction before delivery.

7.6 Break-Even Analysis

At the full-team operating cost of Rs 3,50,000 per month (average across the year), each active contractor generates Rs 15,360 per month (Rs 192 per hour multiplied by 80 hours). That puts break-even at approximately 23 active contractors at lean-phase costs, or approximately 27 active contractors at full-team costs.

We expect to cross 30 active contractors around Month 7 or 8 from the start of operations. This is consistent with B2B sales timelines in India — the first 3 to 4 months are spent building the platform and pipeline, the next 2 to 3 months on closing first clients and ramping contractors. Operational break-even should be reached by the end of Month 8. The total investment of Rs 25 lakhs is expected to be recovered from accumulated profits by Month 14 to 16 (early Year 2).

CHAPTER 8: RISKS, OPPORTUNITIES, REWARDS AND SENSITIVITIES

8.1 Risks and Mitigation Strategies

Technology Risk: The AI interview system might not assess candidates accurately in the early stages, which would hurt client trust. To deal with this, we will run a hybrid model initially — the AI does the first screening and human experts review the edge cases. Client feedback will be used to continuously retrain the model, so accuracy improves as we conduct more interviews.

Client Acquisition Risk: We are a new company with no track record, and enterprises are cautious about trying unknown vendors. Our response is the free pilot program — clients can try 2 contractors for 2 weeks at no cost. We will also target smaller startups and AI companies first since they make decisions faster. Building 3 to 5 solid case studies in the first six months is a top priority.

Talent Supply Risk: If not enough skilled professionals join the platform, we cannot fulfil client needs. We are running multiple channels in parallel — campus drives, social media campaigns, a referral program paying Rs 2,000 per referral, and WhatsApp/Telegram communities. India's large pool of professionals actively looking for freelance work gives us a big addressable base.

Cash Flow Risk: We pay contractors monthly but clients might pay late, creating a cash gap. We will require advance deposits from new clients and maintain a six-month working capital reserve. Established clients get net-15 payment terms.

Competition Risk: Bigger platforms with more money could add similar features. Our focus on AI data services with verified domain experts is a niche that generalist platforms are unlikely to prioritize. And once clients integrate our processes into their workflows, switching becomes inconvenient for them.

Regulatory Risk: Changes in gig worker classification laws or data privacy regulations (like the Digital Personal Data Protection Act) could increase compliance costs. We plan to have a legal advisor from day one and design our contracts to be flexible.

8.2 Opportunities

The AI industry globally is growing fast, and the need for human experts in model training and evaluation is only increasing. India has a 56.35 percent employability rate and about 16 percent of the world's AI talent. As remote work becomes more normal, Indian professionals become more accessible to global companies.

The AI interview engine we build for ourselves also has standalone commercial value. Recruitment agencies, corporate HR teams, and educational institutions could all use an AI-powered screening tool. This is a significant revenue opportunity we plan to explore in Year 2.

Government programs like Startup India offer real benefits — a three-year tax holiday for eligible startups, incubation support, and access to funding. These can meaningfully reduce our costs in the early stage.

And perhaps most importantly, every interview we conduct and every project we deliver generates data that makes our systems better. Someone starting from scratch next year would be that much further behind.

8.3 Rewards

For the founders, this is a chance to build a technology business in a sector that is clearly growing. With consistent execution, the company could reach a meaningful valuation within three years.

For contractors, the platform gives access to better-paying, more structured work than what informal freelance channels offer. A doctor doing AI evaluation work through us could earn considerably more per hour than through traditional routes.

For clients, we offer pre-tested talent delivered in days rather than months, at a fraction of what Western hiring costs.

For the broader economy, TalentLens creates a formal channel for India's skilled professionals to participate in the global AI industry, contributing to employment, skill building, and foreign exchange.

8.4 Sensitivity Analysis

| Scenario | What changes | What happens |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Conservative | We reach only 120 active contractors by year end instead of 200 | Revenue drops to about Rs 65 lakhs. Still covers costs in H2. Break-even shifts to Month 9-10. Business survives on working capital reserve. |
| Aggressive | We reach 350 active contractors by year end | Revenue rises to about Rs 1.8 crores. Need to hire 2-3 more people earlier. Significantly higher profit. Seed round in Year 2 becomes attractive. |

| Delayed First Client | First paying client comes in Month 6 instead of Month 4-5 | Revenue shifts 2 months later, Year 1 total drops to about Rs 60-70 lakhs. Working capital covers the gap but gets tight. May need to cut spending or raise a small bridge. |

The main takeaway is that the business does not depend on everything going right. Operating costs are low enough that even in delayed or conservative scenarios, the working capital reserve carries us through Year 1. The commission model means every contractor who starts billing immediately contributes to covering costs. The real risk is not costs — it is how fast we can land clients.

CONCLUSION

TalentLens is positioned where three things come together: the AI industry's growing need for human expertise, India's gig economy expanding at 21 percent per year, and a talent-matching system that leaves 82 percent of employers unable to find the people they need.

The market is large and growing. No existing competitor combines AI-based skill verification, a managed marketplace, and specialized AI data services in one place. Our strategy of objective AI screening, domain expert specialization, and India-first cost advantage gives us a clear way to stand out. The founding team of seven is lean and focused, backed by advisory and institutional partnerships. The financials are conservative — recurring commission revenue, break-even at around 27 to 30 active contractors, and projected Year 1 net profit of about Rs 48 lakhs on an investment of Rs 25 lakhs, with significant room for upside if client acquisition moves faster than expected. The opportunities ahead are considerably larger than the risks we face.

While TalentLens will create meaningful work for hundreds of Indian professionals in its first year and thousands in the years after, our investors can expect steady returns from a business model that improves with every interview conducted and every project delivered. Together, we can bring about real change in how India's talent connects with the world's most important technology companies.
